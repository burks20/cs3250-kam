Abstract

The U.S. public is now aware, more than ever, of the disparity in race of those who are prosecuted as criminals in the U.S. justice system. This inequality is defined as having a higher ratio of ethnic groups within the penal system as opposed to their ratio in the general populace. However, undue racial disparity stems from unequal procedures in circumstances. This racial disparity can be a result of criminally unrelated characteristics that are used as a bias for profiling, arrests, arraignment, and imprisonment. Agendas of certain agencies can also create a bias in arrests, such as the “War on Drugs” that tends to affect ethnic individuals in downtrodden socioeconomic areas. Although other factors such as mandatory minimum sentences for certain crimes, unequal access to resources, and individual factors per case (attorneys, judges, administrators) may contribute to racial disparity in the justice system, this study will try to find a correlation among 1-to-1 and many-to-1 attributes.

Data will be analyzed on two fronts. Due to the abundance of data provided by the state of Connecticut, a limited scope analysis will be conducted for the individual state of Connecticut. The state provides datasets of those awaiting pre-trial arraignment, which includes their charge, bond amount, and ethnicity. A dataset is also given for those who have been imprisoned (including their ethnicity), as well as a dataset on geographical location with arrest numbers. These data feeds were made publicly available on http://ct.gov as flat files of CSV, XML, JSON, and others. Pre-trial records include several hundred cases starting in July of 2016, while other datasets also include a few hundred rows of data. Each county’s socioeconomic data is also available on the local state’s website.

On a larger scope, datasets from the Department of Justice, published on http://data.gov, will be used for year-to-year analysis on arrests. These flat files include race, age, facility, offense, prosecuting agencies, arresting agencies, and metadata on the incarcerated. Some records are only viewable through limited data view models on the DoJ website and may need to be scraped using a client-side script. Datasets range from a dozen entries per set to tens of thousands per set.

Since one of the leading factors in racial disparity can be geographical bias, the dataset from Connecticut will be used for the small-scale analysis based on economic differences of counties. On the other hand, a dynamic graph could show the charges among those arrested at a federal and local level, comparing charges to the arresting organization (local police, DEA, ICE, etc.) and their publicly stated missions/agendas at the time of arrest. Predicting criminal behavior (year-to-year) between plots using unrelated personal data vs. pure crime records would be possible with these datasets; a significant outcome in predictability of arrests using pure criminal history could indicate that racial profiling (using unrelated conducts such as unemployment, education, finances, etc.) could be an inadequate model of predicting future criminal activity.
